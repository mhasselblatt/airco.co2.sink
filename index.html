🔬 Physics fact — heat pumps and the outdoor environment
Does the outdoor unit heat the neighbourhood? Negligibly — and it is not a net warming source.
A heat pump does not create heat — it moves heat from indoors to outdoors. The thermal energy rejected by the outdoor unit was already inside the building; the only net energy added to the environment is the electrical work input (~0.78 kW average). Spread across Basel's urban boundary layer (daytime mixing height >1,000 m), this single unit contributes <0.01% of the city's background anthropogenic heat flux — physically undetectable at neighbourhood scale (Basel BUBBLE experiment, 5–25 W/m² background). Crucially, because this system runs only on solar power during daylight hours, it rejects zero heat at night — when the shallow nocturnal boundary layer (<100 m) would otherwise trap warmth close to the ground. For global climate: moving heat from room air to outdoor air does not increase the total thermal energy of the atmosphere; it is a local redistribution, not a net addition.

The solar export credit — why it tips negative
Surplus PV electricity exported to Basel IWB displaces gas-fired generation in neighbouring European grids. At 10 kWh/day × 60 summer export days × 150 g CO₂/kWh = 90 kg CO₂ avoided per year — far exceeds the 2.9 kg annual gap.
Up to −177 kg/yr net benefit at peak solar export (20 kWh/day)
💨
Portable monobloc AC — the alternative
A single exhaust hose blows hot air out but simultaneously draws warm outside air back in through door gaps, partially cancelling its own cooling. Real-world COP drops to 1.0–1.5 versus 3.85 here. Three units needed over 15 years due to shorter lifespan.
>2,000 kg CO₂ over 15 years · more than 4× the carbon · inferior cooling performance
❌ Common misconception
Air conditioners are bad for the climate — especially in cities.
True for grid-powered monobloc units: poor real-world efficiency, run on fossil electricity, reject heat at night when the shallow nocturnal atmosphere traps it near residents.
